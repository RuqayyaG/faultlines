---
title: "The deal that doesn't change anything"
date: "17.06.2026"
tag: "Macro"
excerpt: "A US-Iran ceasefire memorandum has been framed as a breakthrough, but with energy flows not expected to recover until September, inflation set to stay above target into 2027, and Iran retaining effective control of the Strait, markets are pricing managed uncertainty rather than resolution."
---
A memorandum of understanding signed on June 19 will not reopen the Strait of Hormuz overnight. It will not bring Brent crude back to $69, where it sat at the start of the year. And it will not undo what the IEA has called the largest oil supply disruption in the history of the global energy market. The G7 summit deal is a political breakthrough. Economically, it is the beginning of a very long road back.



**The numbers tell the story**. When the US-Israeli campaign against Iran began on February 28, Brent crude surged 65% in a single month, its largest monthly rise on record. By mid-April, WTI peaked at $94 per barrel. The World Bank's April forecast had Brent averaging $86 a barrel across 2026, up from $69 in 2025, but that projection is already being revised down; Brent was trading around $79 on Wednesday as deal optimism pushed prices to a three-month low. They may not be. The MOU is a 60-day extension of a ceasefire, *not* a resolution. Iran's nuclear programme was explicitly set aside for further negotiations.



The Strait of Hormuz handles roughly 20% of the world's oil trade and 20% of its LNG. Closing it, even partially, was enough to trigger what economists at CEPR describe as two to three times larger a disruption than any previous geopolitical oil supply shock, eclipsing 1973 and 1990. The inflationary consequences are still working through the system. Capital Economics estimates energy flows will reach 80% of prewar levels by September at the earliest. Inflation is set to stay above target in most major economies throughout 2026 and into the first half of 2027, even under the optimistic scenario.



**The geopolitical picture complicates the economic one.** Iran and Oman would manage traffic through the strait under the MOU's terms. The White House dismissed that framing as a "complete fabrication." Amos Hochstein, Biden's former senior energy adviser, was blunt: "No matter what happens, the Iranians will control the Strait of Hormuz for the foreseeable future." Markets, which had priced in a clean resolution, are now repricing the gap between a ceasefire and an actual settlement.



The asymmetry of exposure matters for investors. Asia absorbs roughly 84% of the crude and LNG that transits the strait. China, India, Japan and South Korea account for 70% of oil flows alone. Europe's exposure is significant but secondary. The UK faces a particular squeeze on fertiliser imports, with over 30% of global urea passing through the Strait. Food price inflation was already embedded before the war; disruption to planting-season supply chains will push it higher through year-end.
What markets are pricing now is managed uncertainty, not resolution. The deal is real. So are its limits.
